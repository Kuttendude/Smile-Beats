Background Research & Problem Definition

Understanding the problem before designing a solution.

← Back to Home

Problem Definition

A majority of young children fail to brush their teeth for the dentist-recommended two minutes, leading to preventable oral health issues such as cavities and long-term dental problems. Parents often struggle to enforce consistent brushing habits without conflict, highlighting the need for a solution that makes brushing engaging, structured, and independent for children.

Relevant Statistics

  • 21% of children ages 6–11 develop cavities in their permanent teeth.
  • Over 50% of children between ages 6–9 have experienced at least one cavity.
  • Across the United States, children collectively miss over 51 million hours of school each year due to dental-related illnesses.

These statistics highlight how common and impactful poor brushing habits are among children. Without proper routines, small issues like inconsistent brushing can quickly develop into serious health and academic problems.

Why Kids Don’t Brush Their Teeth

  • Many children see brushing as a chore rather than an important habit.
  • Some do not fully understand the long-term health consequences of poor oral hygiene.
  • Others avoid brushing because it feels uncomfortable or unpleasant.
  • Many children skip brushing due to laziness or busy routines, especially in the morning or before bed.

These behavioral patterns show that the problem is not just about knowledge, but about motivation and engagement. Any successful solution must make brushing feel less like a task and more like an enjoyable routine.

Target Market Demographics

Our primary target users are children ages 3–10 who are still developing daily hygiene habits. Secondary users include parents and caregivers who are responsible for enforcing brushing routines. Additional stakeholders include pediatric dentists and educators who promote proper oral hygiene practices.

User Survey Results

  • 100% of surveyed parents reported that they still need to monitor or remind their child to brush their teeth.
  • 86% of parents said brushing is a daily struggle or requires consistent effort.
  • 71% of parents rely on reminders, routines, or incentives to get their child to brush.
  • Most parents reported that children struggle to brush properly without guidance.

These results show that brushing is not an independent habit for most children. Instead, it requires constant supervision, reminders, or external motivation. This highlights a clear need for a solution that makes brushing more engaging and helps children develop consistent habits on their own.

Proper Brushing Expectations

  • Brush teeth twice a day (morning and night)
  • Brush for a full two minutes
  • Use proper technique, including brushing all teeth and gums

While these expectations are clear, both research and survey data show that most children do not consistently meet them without help. This gap between expectations and reality is what our solution aims to address.

Key Insights

Time is critical

Children rarely meet the 2-minute requirement, directly impacting oral health.

Engagement drives behavior

Making brushing interactive and enjoyable significantly increases consistency.

Parents need better tools

Parents want solutions that reduce conflict and promote independence.
